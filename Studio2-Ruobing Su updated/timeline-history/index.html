Chinatown Branch Library

Historical timeline

1896 - 1915

BPL is founded by the Commonwealth of Massachusetts, as the first public library system in the U.S. The Tyler street branch library served as a BPL Delivery Station on 130 Tyler Street, and was officially declared as a branch library of BPL in 1915. And the branch library moved into a new municipal buidling at 118 Tyler Street (now the site of Tai Tung Village)


Image Courtsey of Boston Public Library.

1938

When BPL announced the closure of Tyler Street branch, there was a protest organized by city councilor John Fitzgerald, Shibley Malouf of Morgan Memorial, William Moy of Chinese Merchants' Association and some people from other organizations. Two hundred children were holding signs read "No Library, no schools" and "Closed library, closed minds."


Image Courtsey of Boston Public Library.

1951

The branch library reopened as a demand from the community as a reading room.


Inside of Tyler Street Branch library, 1916-1930. Image Courtsey of Boston Public Library.

1956

Boston Redevelopment Authority began the Central Artery construction in 1954, which would cut through Chinatown, including where the library located. The library went down, despite the fact that the city did not end up using the space for the highway. Tai-tung Village, an affordable residential building was built on the site where the library used to locate.


Boston's Chinatown during Central Artery project. Image Courtsey of Leslie Jones.

1960 - 1970

Book mobile comes through Chinatown once a week. Accoring to mant of the residents that remmeber, the van was also very inconsistent and did not seem to adhere to a proper schedule. Parents and kids found it unrealiable. (Boston City Council Hearing, 2006)


Boston Public Library book mobile truck. Image Courtsey of Boston Public Library.

2001

"The Friends of the Chinatown Library (FOCL) launches. Youth group of Chinatown residents took the lead of the campagin by starting the community survey of library service."


Youth from Chinese Progressive Association worked with Friends of Chinatown Library together on community surveys on the library service.Image Courtsey of Friends of Chinatown Library.

2009 - 2013

The Storefront Library opened as a reponse to City Councilor Sam Yoon and Leslie Davol. However, it was shut down only after three months becasue of budgeting reasons. Following that, a reading room opened at Oak Terrace and closes in 2013.

Image Courtsey of Street Lab.

2014-2015

CYI Library Committee met with Boston City Councilors for a Chinatown Branch Library. CYI partnered with FOCL to conduct focus groups to develop a community vision for a Chinatown Branch Library.

Image Courtsey of Bay State Banner.

2016

City of Boston funds 2nd feasibility study for a branch library in Chinatown.

2017

The site reviewing for the temporary branch began in the summer and the contruction began right after the decision. BPL held three community meetings and presentations in order to gather information from the residents of Chinatown. By the end of 2017, BPL published the final fesaibility study.

Boston mayor Marty Walsh visiting Chinatown branch library temporary site's construction.
Image Courtsey of US China Press.

2018

The temporary site is located at the basement of China Trade Center, which was owned by Boston Planning and Development Agency. The branch is 1,500 square feet and a capacity of 150 people. The library is equipped with bilingual staff and with some special programs in Madarain and Cnatonese.

Image Courtsey of Ruobing Su.

Future

In FY19 BPL will begin reviewing viable sites for a permanent location for library services in Chinatown. The FY19 budget includes $100,000 for the review, and will use the Chinatown Library Services Planning Study, completed in 2017, and feedback from the recently opened temporary space to inform the process.
